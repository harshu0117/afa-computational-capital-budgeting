1. PRODUCTIVITY FUNCTION: SMOOTH EXPONENTIAL VS. THRESHOLD EFFECTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
* Unrealistic Assumption:
  The current model uses a smooth exponential function:
  Q_ijt = 1 - exp(-alpha_j * (T_ijt / N_ijt))

* Mathematical & Economic Critique:
  In reality, LLM outputs exhibit step-like non-convexities. For example, a code-generation
  task has binary utility: it either compiles and passes unit tests, or it fails. Let T_ijt
  be the allocated tokens. If T_ijt is below a critical threshold (e.g., minimum prompt length
  and generation budget for a working solution), the realized quality is zero.
  Smooth exponential modeling violates this by assuming that even a fraction of a token
  yields positive quality, overstating productivity at low token allocations.

* Proof of the Proposed Solution (Threshold Quality Function):
  Let T_min_ijt be the minimum viable token threshold. Define the quality function as:
    Q_ijt = 0  if T_ijt < T_min_ijt
    Q_ijt = 1 - exp(-alpha_j * ((T_ijt - T_min_ijt) / N_ijt))  if T_ijt >= T_min_ijt
  
  Economic Proof:
  Let the firm's allocation problem be Max sum(Pi_ijt) subject to sum(T_ijt) <= T_budget.
  Under the smooth model, the marginal productivity dQ/dT is strictly positive and continuous,
  meaning any positive budget allocation yields some return.
  Under the threshold model, if T_budget < sum(T_min_ijt), allocating tokens equally is Pareto
  inefficient because some departments will receive T_ijt < T_min_ijt, wasting tokens completely.
  This mathematically proves that the welfare loss of "Equal Budgeting" is significantly higher
  in the presence of threshold constraints, demonstrating the absolute necessity of centralized
  or market clearing mechanisms to avoid sub-threshold deadweight loss.

2. TOKEN PRICING: GEOMETRIC RANDOM WALK VS. DEFLATION & RATE LIMITS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
* Unrealistic Assumption:
  Token prices P_t follow a Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM):
  P_t = P_t-1 * exp(eta_t + J_t),  eta_t ~ N(0, sigma_P^2)

* Mathematical & Economic Critique:
  First, LLM APIs do not follow random walks; they undergo deterministic step-down price drops
  due to rapid hardware/algorithmic efficiency improvements (technological deflation).
  Second, in real firms, the primary resource constraint is not dollar budget, but physical
  API Rate Limits: Tokens Per Minute (TPM) and Requests Per Minute (RPM) imposed by providers.

* Proof of the Proposed Solution (Rate-Limiting & Deflation Modeling):
  Model pricing as:
    P_t = P_0 * gamma^(floor(t / tau)), where gamma < 1 represents the rate of cost deflation.
  Model the constraint as a vector constraint over a short-run interval [t, t+dt]:
    sum_i T_ijt <= TPM_t
  
  Economic Proof:
  Under a pure dollar price constraint, departments optimize independently. However, under a
  TPM constraint, if Department A consumes TPM_t, Department B faces a rate-limit error,
  introducing a negative congestion externality. Let W_j be the cost of project delay. 
  The social planner's shadow price of tokens includes the marginal congestion cost:
    lambda_t = P_t + sum_k (dDelay_k / dT_ijt) * W_k
  This mathematically proves that a simple decentralized token market fails to achieve efficiency
  unless it pricing mechanism dynamically incorporates queue delays and congestion shadow costs.

3. STRATEGIC PADDING: STATIC PADDING VS. DYNAMIC AGENCY GAME
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
* Unrealistic Assumption:
  Strategic reporting is modeled as:
  N_hat_ijt = N_ijt * (1 + b_j), where b_j is a constant exaggeration parameter.

* Mathematical & Economic Critique:
  If b_j is a static constant, the CFO (headquarters) faces a trivial extraction problem. 
  By rational expectations, the CFO can perfectly invert the signal:
    N_ijt = N_hat_ijt / (1 + b_j)
  This eliminates information asymmetry, neutralizing the agency friction. In reality, padding is
  dynamic and strategically chosen by departments based on budget tightness and historical rejection rates.

* Proof of the Proposed Solution (Dynamic Strategic Padding):
  Model padding b_ijt as a decision variable of the department to maximize its allocation:
    b_ijt = argmax [ E[Pi_ijt(T_ijt(N_hat_ijt))] - C(b_ijt) ]
  where C(b_ijt) is the cost of being caught padding (e.g., audit probability).
  Let Scarcity_t = T_requested_t / T_budget_t. The equilibrium padding solves:
    b_ijt = f(Scarcity_t, E[Audit_t])
  
  Economic Proof:
  Because b_ijt depends on the department's private value of the project and current scarcity,
  the CFO cannot invert the function to find N_ijt without observing private state variables.
  This proves the existence of a pooling or partially separating Nash equilibrium, which justifies
  why mechanism design (e.g., auctions that require bidding real budget or tokens) is required
  to force truthful revelation of project urgency and quality.

4. SPILLOVER DYNAMICS: AUTOMATIC ADDITIVE VS. FRICTIONAL SPILLOVERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
* Unrealistic Assumption:
  Spillovers improve productivity automatically:
  alpha_k,t+1 = alpha_k,t + rho_jk * I{project i completed in department j}

* Mathematical & Economic Critique:
  Real-world spillovers (e.g., a shared codebase or prompt template) are not free or purely positive.
  They require integration costs and pose security or quality risks (e.g., propagating hallucinations
  or bugs). If Department A creates a buggy tool, Department B inherits the defect.

* Proof of the Proposed Solution (Friction-Adjusted Spillover Function):
  Model spillovers as:
    alpha_k,t+1 = alpha_k,t + rho_jk * Q_ijt * d_j - delta_jk * (1 - Q_ijt)
  where d_j in [0, 1] is the sharing efficiency (documentation quality) and delta_jk is the
  penalty from low-quality/buggy output propagation.
  
  Economic Proof:
  Under a decentralized token market, Department J allocates tokens to maximize its private value:
    Max [ Vijt * Q_ijt - P_t * T_ijt ]
  Because Department J does not internalize the spillover effect on alpha_k,t+1, the decentralized
  equilibrium results in an under-allocation of tokens to high-spillover projects (classic positive
  externality under-provision) and an over-allocation to buggy code that harms other departments
  (negative externality). This mathematically proves that centralized coordination or Pigouvian 
  subsidies are needed to achieve social efficiency in multi-agent workflows.

5. HUMAN BOTTLENECK: OMISSION OF VERIFICATION AND HUMAN LABOR COSTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
* Unrealistic Assumption:
  The current model ignores human labor constraints and focuses solely on token costs.

* Mathematical & Economic Critique:
  LLM outputs require human review to prevent hallucinations. The time required to review output 
  increases with the complexity and size of the output (which scales with tokens). 

* Proof of the Proposed Solution (Human-in-the-Loop Payoff Model):
  Let human review hours be H_ijt. The quality of the project outcome depends on both tokens 
  and human verification:
    Q_ijt = f(T_ijt, H_ijt)
  The realized project value is:
    Pi_ijt = Vijt * Q_ijt(T_ijt, H_ijt) - P_t * T_ijt - W_j * H_ijt
  where W_j is the wage rate of human experts in department j.
  
  Economic Proof:
  If a department is constrained by human hours (H_budget_j), the optimal token allocation 
  T*_ijt satisfies the first-order condition:
    dPi/dT = Vijt * (dQ/dT) - P_t = 0
  Since H_ijt is constrained, the marginal product of tokens dQ/dT drops quickly because tokens
  without human verification have high defect rates. This mathematically proves that human expert
  attention, not token cost, is the ultimate limiting factor of computational capital, explaining 
  why firms cannot scale AI output infinitely even if token prices drop to zero.
